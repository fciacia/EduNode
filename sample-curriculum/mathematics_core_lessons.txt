MATHEMATICS — CORE LESSONS (Grades 6–8)

Place Value
In our number system every digit has a place value that depends on its position.
Reading from the right, the places are ones, tens, hundreds, thousands, and so on,
each ten times larger than the place to its right. In the number 3 472 the 3 means
three thousands, the 4 means four hundreds, the 7 means seven tens, and the 2 means
two ones. Understanding place value lets us compare numbers, line them up to add and
subtract, and understand decimals, where places to the right of the point are tenths,
hundredths and thousandths.

Fractions
A fraction shows a part of a whole. It is written as a numerator over a denominator,
such as 3/4. The denominator tells how many equal parts the whole is divided into,
and the numerator tells how many of those parts we take. Fractions with the same
value, like 1/2 and 2/4, are called equivalent fractions. To add or subtract
fractions they must have the same denominator; we rewrite them using a common
denominator first. A fraction whose numerator is smaller than its denominator is a
proper fraction; if it is larger, it is an improper fraction.

Decimals
A decimal is another way to write a fraction whose denominator is ten, a hundred, a
thousand, and so on. The digits after the decimal point show tenths, hundredths and
thousandths. For example, 0.75 means seventy-five hundredths, which is the same as
the fraction 3/4. To add or subtract decimals we line up the decimal points so that
each place value matches. Decimals make it easy to work with money and measurements.

Percentages
A percentage is a fraction out of one hundred; the word percent means "per hundred".
The symbol is %. So 50% means 50 out of 100, which equals the fraction 1/2 or the
decimal 0.5. To find a percentage of an amount, change the percent to a decimal and
multiply. For example, 20% of 60 is 0.2 times 60, which is 12. Percentages are used
for discounts, test scores, and interest.

Ratio and Proportion
A ratio compares two quantities of the same kind, written with a colon, such as 2:3.
A ratio of 2:3 of boys to girls means that for every 2 boys there are 3 girls. We can
simplify ratios by dividing both numbers by a common factor, just like fractions. A
proportion is a statement that two ratios are equal, such as 2:3 = 4:6. Ratios help us
share amounts fairly and scale recipes or maps up and down.

Integers
Integers are the whole numbers together with their negatives and zero: …, -3, -2, -1,
0, 1, 2, 3, … Negative numbers describe things below a reference point, such as
temperatures below zero or money owed. On a number line, numbers increase to the right
and decrease to the left, so -5 is less than -1. Adding a positive number moves right;
adding a negative number moves left. Two negatives multiplied together give a positive.

Introduction to Algebra
Algebra uses letters, called variables, to stand for unknown numbers. An expression
like 2x + 3 means "two times a number, plus three". An equation says that two
expressions are equal, such as 2x + 3 = 11. To solve it we do the same operation to
both sides to keep the balance: subtract 3 from both sides to get 2x = 8, then divide
both sides by 2 to find x = 4. Algebra lets us describe patterns and solve problems
where a quantity is unknown.

Perimeter and Area
The perimeter of a shape is the total distance around its edge, found by adding the
lengths of all the sides. The area is the amount of flat space the shape covers,
measured in square units. The area of a rectangle is length times width. The area of a
triangle is one half times the base times the height. For example, a rectangle 5 cm by
3 cm has a perimeter of 16 cm and an area of 15 square centimetres.

Angles
An angle is the amount of turn between two lines that meet at a point, measured in
degrees. A right angle is exactly 90 degrees and is marked with a small square. An
acute angle is less than 90 degrees, and an obtuse angle is between 90 and 180 degrees.
A straight line forms an angle of 180 degrees. The three angles inside any triangle
always add up to 180 degrees.

The Pythagorean Theorem
A right-angled triangle has one angle of 90 degrees. The longest side, opposite the
right angle, is called the hypotenuse. The Pythagorean theorem states that the square
of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides: a² + b² = c².
For example, a triangle with shorter sides 3 and 4 has a hypotenuse of 5, because
9 + 16 = 25, and the square root of 25 is 5.

Averages
An average is a single value that represents a set of numbers. The mean is found by
adding all the values and dividing by how many there are. The median is the middle
value when the numbers are placed in order. The mode is the value that appears most
often. For the numbers 2, 4, 4, 6 and 9, the mean is 5, the median is 4, and the mode
is 4. Averages help us summarise and compare data.

Probability
Probability measures how likely an event is to happen, on a scale from 0 to 1. A
probability of 0 means impossible and a probability of 1 means certain. For equally
likely outcomes, the probability equals the number of favourable outcomes divided by
the total number of outcomes. When tossing a fair coin there are two outcomes, so the
probability of heads is 1/2. When rolling a six-sided die, the probability of getting a
4 is 1/6.
