SCIENCE — PHOTOSYNTHESIS (LIGHT REACTIONS)

How Light Starts Photosynthesis
Photosynthesis begins when pigments in a plant absorb light. The sun gives off
many kinds of light, but plants can only use a narrow range of it. Plants absorb
light with wavelengths between about 400 and 700 nanometres, which scientists
call photosynthetically active radiation. The green pigment chlorophyll absorbs
blue and red light but reflects green light, which is why leaves look green.

The Photosystems
The change from light energy into chemical energy happens in protein groups
called photosystems, found in the thylakoid membrane inside the leaf cells. There
are two photosystems, called Photosystem II and Photosystem I. Light-harvesting
groups with hundreds of chlorophyll molecules pass the captured energy along until
it reaches a special pair of chlorophyll molecules at the reaction centre.

Making Energy for the Plant
Photosystem II takes electrons from water molecules, while Photosystem I helps
make a carrier molecule called NADPH. As the electrons move along a chain, they
push hydrogen ions to one side of the membrane. The ions then flow back through a
protein called ATP synthase, and this flow makes ATP. ATP and NADPH are the
energy-rich products the plant uses to build sugar.
