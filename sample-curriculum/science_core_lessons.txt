SCIENCE — CORE LESSONS (Grades 6–8)

Living Cells
All living things are made of tiny units called cells, which are the building blocks
of life. Some living things are a single cell, while plants and animals are made of
billions. Every cell has a thin membrane that holds it together and controls what
enters and leaves, a jelly-like cytoplasm where activities happen, and a nucleus that
acts as the control centre. Plant cells also have a stiff cell wall for support, a
large vacuole storing water, and green chloroplasts that capture sunlight.

Photosynthesis and Respiration
Green plants make their own food by photosynthesis. In the leaves, chlorophyll
captures sunlight and uses its energy to turn water and carbon dioxide into glucose, a
sugar, releasing oxygen as a by-product. Respiration is almost the reverse process:
living cells break down glucose using oxygen to release the energy they need to grow
and move, giving out carbon dioxide and water. Plants photosynthesise in the light but
respire all the time, day and night.

The Human Digestive System
Digestion breaks down the food we eat into small pieces the body can absorb. It begins
in the mouth, where teeth chew food and saliva starts to break down starch. Food then
travels down the food pipe to the stomach, where acids and juices continue digestion.
In the small intestine the digested nutrients pass into the blood, and in the large
intestine water is absorbed before the waste leaves the body. A balanced diet keeps the
system healthy.

States of Matter
Matter exists in three common states: solid, liquid and gas. In a solid the particles
are packed closely and only vibrate, so a solid keeps its shape. In a liquid the
particles are close but can slide past one another, so a liquid flows and takes the
shape of its container. In a gas the particles move quickly and spread out to fill all
the space available. Heating gives particles more energy and can melt a solid or boil a
liquid; cooling reverses these changes.

The Water Cycle
The water cycle describes how water moves around the Earth and is never used up. The
Sun heats oceans, rivers and lakes, and the water evaporates, rising as an invisible
gas called water vapour. High in the cooler sky the vapour condenses into tiny droplets
that form clouds. When the droplets grow heavy they fall as precipitation — rain, snow
or hail. The water flows over land into rivers and back to the sea, and the cycle
begins again.

Forces and Motion
A force is a push or a pull that can make an object start moving, stop, speed up, slow
down or change direction. Forces are measured in units called newtons. When the forces
on an object are balanced, it stays still or keeps moving steadily. When they are
unbalanced, the object changes its motion. Friction is a force that opposes movement
between surfaces, and gravity is the force that pulls objects toward the Earth.

Energy and Its Forms
Energy is the ability to do work or cause change, and it comes in many forms, including
light, heat, sound, electrical, chemical and movement (kinetic) energy. Energy cannot
be created or destroyed; it only changes from one form to another. For example, a torch
changes chemical energy in its battery into light and heat energy. The Sun is the
original source of almost all the energy used by living things on Earth.

Electricity and Circuits
Electricity is a flow of tiny charged particles called electrons. For a current to
flow, electrons need a complete loop called a circuit, usually made of a cell, wires
and a component such as a bulb. If the loop is broken by an open switch, the current
stops and the bulb goes out. Materials that let current pass through, like metals, are
conductors; materials that block it, like plastic and rubber, are insulators.

Acids and Bases
Acids and bases are two groups of chemicals with opposite properties. Acids taste sour,
like lemon juice and vinegar, while bases feel slippery and include soap and baking
soda. We test them using an indicator such as litmus paper: acids turn blue litmus red,
and bases turn red litmus blue. When an acid and a base are mixed in the right amounts
they cancel each other out in a reaction called neutralisation.

Ecosystems and Food Chains
An ecosystem is a community of living things together with the non-living surroundings
they depend on, such as soil, water and air. Within it, energy passes along a food
chain. Green plants are producers that make their own food. Animals that eat plants are
herbivores, and animals that eat other animals are carnivores. A food chain might run
from grass to grasshopper to frog to snake. Decomposers like fungi break down dead
matter and return nutrients to the soil.

The Solar System
The Solar System is made up of the Sun and everything that orbits it, held in place by
gravity. Eight planets travel around the Sun in nearly circular paths: Mercury, Venus,
Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune. The four inner planets are small and
rocky, while the four outer planets are large and made mostly of gas. The Earth takes
one year to orbit the Sun and spins once a day, which gives us day and night.

Light and Sound
Light is a form of energy that travels in straight lines and lets us see. It travels
extremely fast and can be reflected by mirrors or bent when it passes into water. White
light is actually a mixture of colours, which we see when a rainbow forms. Sound is
made by vibrations that travel as waves through air, water or solids. Unlike light,
sound cannot travel through empty space because it needs particles to carry it.
