What is a fraction? (Mathematics)
A fraction represents parts of a whole thing or quantity that has been divided into equal pieces. Imagine you have something like a pizza cut into slices; each slice would be considered a part, which we can write as a fraction. The number on top (numerator) tells us how many slices are being talked about, and the bottom number (denominator) shows into how many total pieces that whole thing has been divided. For example, if you have one pizza cut into four equal parts and take two of those parts to eat, this situation is written as a fraction: 2/4 or "two-fourths." This means out of the entirety (the whole), we are considering just part of it—specifically, two pieces.
expected concepts: part, whole, numerator, denominator
What does the denominator of a fraction tell you? (Mathematics)
The denominator in a fraction tells us into how many equal parts something is divided. For example, if we have one pizza and cut it into four pieces (or quarters), each piece represents a part written as a fraction with 1 on top for the number of slices you get or take, and '4' at the bottom telling that there are four such equally-sized parts in total. So denominator helps us understand not just how many but also into what kind size pieces we’re talking about when sharing things like pizza among friends!
expected concepts: equal, parts, divided
What is a proper fraction? (Mathematics)
A proper fraction in simple terms is like when we have less than a whole pizza and the number of slices (numerator) isn't enough to make up that entire pizza, but not zero either. Imagine you only got one slice out of four pieces - this would be written as 1/4 or 'one-fourth'. It means we have less than a whole thing because when the top number is smaller compared to the bottom number (denominator), like in our pizza example, it's called a proper fraction.
expected concepts: numerator, smaller, denominator
What are equivalent fractions? (Mathematics)
Equivalent fractions represent the same amount or value but look different. For example, if you have half an apple (which is like saying 2 out of 4 pieces), it's also called two-fourths because four parts make a whole and we took two of them to eat. So, 1/2 and 2/4 are equivalent fractions; they mean the same thing even though they look different!
expected concepts: same, value
What is a mixed number? (Mathematics)
A mixed number is like a regular whole number and a little extra bit of a proper fraction combined into one. Imagine you have more than two but less than three pieces of something; that's an improper fraction because the top part (numerator) is bigger, right? So if we take our example 8/5 from before, which was an improper fraction when it had to be simplified and divided into whole numbers plus a leftover piece. We can write this as "1 and 3/5", where '1' stands for one full part you get after dividing the pieces evenly, and '3/5' is what’s left over—the extra bit that doesn't make up another whole thing by itself but still counts towards your total. So a mixed number helps us picture how much we really have in terms of both wholes and parts!
expected concepts: whole, fraction
What is place value? (Mathematics)
Place value in mathematics tells us what each digit represents based on where it stands in a number. Imagine you're looking at numbers from right to left; starting with ones, then tens, hundreds and so forth, every spot holds ten times the worth of its position to the right. In simpler words, if we have 3472 for example, '3' is actually three thousands because it sits in the thousands place! Knowing this helps us understand larger numbers better when adding or comparing them. It also works with decimals after a point where each spot becomes ten times smaller than its neighbor to the left of the decimal.
expected concepts: digit, position, place
What is a decimal? (Mathematics)
A decimal is like a special kind of fraction where the bottom number (the denominator) can be tenths, hundredths or even thousandths. It's written with numbers and points in between them, just like when we write money amounts! For example, if you have $0.75, that means 75 out of every 100 cents – which is the same as having three quarters (3/4) of a dollar. When adding or subtracting decimals, make sure to line up their points so each place matches before doing any math!
expected concepts: tenths, hundredths, point
How do you add two fractions with different denominators? (Mathematics)
To add two fractions that have different denominators, first find a common denominator for both numbers—this will be their least common multiple (LCM). Once found, rewrite each fraction so they both have this new denominator. Then, adjust the numerators accordingly to keep the value of the fractions unchanged and finally add them together while keeping the same denominator. For example, if you want to add 1/2 + 1/3: - The LCM for 2 and 3 is 6, so we rewrite both as equivalent fractions with this common denominator: (1 x 3)/(2 x 3) becomes 3/6, and (1 x 2)/(3 x 2) becomes 2/6. - Now add the numerators of these new fractions while keeping the same denominator to get 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6 as our answer.
expected concepts: common, denominator

What is a cell? (Science)
A cell is like a tiny room inside your body or any living thing that helps it grow and stay alive. It's so small you can only see cells with powerful microscopes! Inside every cell are parts called the membrane, cytoplasm, and nucleus. The membrane keeps everything in and out; think of it like a security guard at the door. Cytoplasm is jelly-like stuff where tiny activities happen inside the cell. And the nucleus? It's kind of like the boss room that controls what goes on with these parts, especially when cells make new ones or grow bigger. Some special plant and animal cells have unique features too!
expected concepts: living, building, membrane
What does the nucleus of a cell do? (Science)
The nucleus acts like the boss or control center of the cell. It holds something called DNA, which is like an instruction book that tells the cell how to work and grow. The nucleus sends out instructions for making new parts inside the cell, too. So it's really important because without its job, a cell wouldn't know what to do or when to stop growing! What is respiration? (Science)
Note: this is not in your textbook — here is a general explanation: Respiration is like breathing for our bodies, but it's also a way to make energy from the food we eat. When you breathe out air that has carbon dioxide in it and take in fresh oxygen-rich air when you breathe in, your body uses this process called respiration. Inside each cell of your body, tiny parts work like little machines using glucose (a kind of sugar) from food along with the oxygen to create energy for our cells while making water and carbon dioxide as waste products that we then get rid of when we breathe out or sweat. It's an essential process all living things need, including us!

Digestion begins in the mouth where our teeth chew up the food into smaller pieces making it easier to swallow. At the same time, saliva from glands near your mouth starts breaking down starches found in foods like bread and pasta right there before they go on their journey through our body!

What gas do plants release during photosynthesis? (Science)
During photosynthesis, which is how green plants make their own food using sunlight, water from the soil, and carbon dioxide from the air, they also produce a gas called oxygen. This happens inside the leaves where chlorophyll captures energy from sunlight to convert these ingredients into glucose (a type of sugar that serves as plant's food) in specialized structures within leaf cells known as reaction centers. As part of this process, plants release oxygen gas back into the air for us and other animals to breathe. This is a very important function because it helps maintain the balance of gases on Earth!

What is the water cycle? (Science)
The water cycle shows how water moves around our planet without running out. The Sun heats up bodies of water like oceans, rivers, and lakes, causing them to evaporate into invisible gas called water vapour that rises in the air. Up high where it's cooler, this vapour turns back into tiny droplets forming clouds through a process known as condensation. When these droplets get too heavy, they fall down from the sky to Earth as precipitation like rain or snow. This water then collects in bodies of water and starts evaporating again when it gets warm enough under the Sun's heat, continuing this cycle over and over without any loss of water on our planet.
expected concepts: water, moves

What extra parts does a plant cell have? (Science)
A plant cell has some special features not found in animal cells or even human cells like us. It's got this strong outer wall called a 'cell wall', which helps it keep its shape and protect the inside of the cell. Inside, there are also large storage spaces known as vacuoles that hold water, nutrients, and waste products. And unlike some animal cells, plant cells have tiny green parts named chloroplasts where they use sunlight to make their own food through a process called 'photosynthesis'. These special features help the cell work better in nature!
expected concepts: cell wall, chloroplast

What is an improper fraction? (Mathematics)
An improper fraction is a type of fraction where the top number (numerator) is greater than or equal to the bottom number (denominator). This means it represents one whole thing and some extra parts too, like having more cookies in your hand than you can eat at once. For example, if we have 7/4 as an improper fraction, this tells us that there are seven pieces of something divided into four equal parts with only three full sets taken. Imagine splitting a pizza into eight slices and taking all the whole ones plus one slice left – you'd write it like 1 and 1/8 or in fractions as 9/8, which is an improper fraction because we have more than four-eighths of that pizza.
expected concepts: numerator, greater, denominator

What is the difference between weather and climate? (Environmental Studies)
Note: this is not in your textbook — here is a general explanation: Weather refers to short-term atmospheric conditions, like rain or sunshine on any given day. It's what you see outside your window right now! Climate, however, describes long-term patterns of weather in a particular area over many years. So if it rains often here for 30 years straight, that’s part of our climate pattern—it tells us about the typical conditions we can expect throughout different seasons and even decades. What is a noun? (English Language)
A noun names people, places, animals, or things. Examples include teacher for person, village for place, dog for animal, or river for thing. Nouns are important because they help us talk about the world around us by identifying different entities in it. In a sentence, you can find nouns that serve as subjects doing an action (like "The cat jumps") and objects receiving an action done to them (such as “She throws the ball”). Remembering these examples will make understanding new words easier!

What is a verb? (English Language)
A verb is an action word that tells us about something happening or being done by someone or something else in the sentence. For example, if I say "The girl dances," 'dance' is the verb because it shows what the girl does. Verbs can also tell when things happen and sometimes how they are being done. They make sentences complete without just having a subject (like who or what) alone. So remember, verbs describe actions like running, eating, reading, growing – anything that tells us about an action in our daily life!
expected concepts: action
How does a sentence begin and end? (English Language)
Note: this is not in your textbook — here is a general explanation: A good way to understand how sentences start and finish is by looking at the parts of speech, which are like building blocks for our words! Let's break it down. Every complete thought or idea we want to share with others starts in what we call 'sentences.' These thoughts begin with a word that tells us who did something (a subject), followed by other words that give more information about the action and sometimes, where things happen (like adverbs). Now for ending sentences. After sharing our complete thought or idea, we signal to stop reading with what's called 'punctuation.' In most cases in English when speaking a full sentence out loud, this is done by using one of these three end marks: the period (.), which looks like two dots side-by-side and means that our thoughts are finished; or if we want to keep talking more about it without stopping, then we use an exclamation mark (!) for excitement. If we're asking someone else a question instead of telling them something straight up, we end with a question mark (?). So remember: Start your sentence by sharing what you know or ask questions; finish sentences using periods to show it’s over, and use exclamation marks when excited about the topic!
expected concepts: capital, full stop
